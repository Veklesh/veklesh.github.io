"Guh!"

With a short scream, Brewitt collapses to the ground.

Fortunately, although it was cold, it wasn’t snowing, so falling to the ground wouldn’t leave him covered in mud—though there was no doubt he’d get dirty from the dirt.

It was hardly the sort of behavior one would expect from the future head of a Count’s household, but Brewitt didn’t seem to care in the least. He stood up and took a fighting stance facing Rei.

Rei had ended up in a mock battle with Brewitt, but no matter how confident Brewitt was in his own skills, compared to Rei—who, since arriving in Elgin, had experienced countless battles… the kind that an ordinary adventurer might encounter only once in a lifetime—there was a huge gap in their strength.

As for Brewitt’s strength, when it came to street fights, he could be called undefeated.

However, that was strictly limited to fights in town or, in some cases, against local soldiers or knights; when it came to facing Rei, the gap in ability was naturally vast.

As if to prove this, even in this mock battle, Rei is facing Brewitt with his bare hands, without his signature weapons—the Death Scythe or the Twilight Spear.

Despite this, Brewitt couldn’t land a single effective blow on Rei and was knocked to the ground time and time again.

Yet Brewitt’s fighting spirit shows no sign of waning… On the contrary, perhaps delighted to be facing Rei—someone he can’t even scratch—he confronts him with a genuinely innocent smile.

“Come onnnnnnnn!”

That said, there was a distinct, systematic quality to the way Brewitt moved.

In other words, this meant Brewitt hadn’t simply taught himself how to fight, but had studied under a teacher.

(Well, looking at Brewitt, you’d forget it, but he is, after all, the heir to the Count’s family… If he was going to train, it makes sense that he’d study under someone of that caliber.)

Rei thought this as he dodged Brewitt’s fists coming at him, but in reality, Rei had never been taught how to handle a spear; he, too, had honed his own style through actual combat.

Moreover, there was virtually no one who could teach him how to use a large scythe like the Death Scythe.

As a result, Rei had honed his dual-spear style through actual combat.

Since a self-taught style isn’t bound by set forms, those who have trained systematically might face unexpected attacks—attacks that deviate from standard tactics—and responding to them is difficult precisely because they know those set forms.

Of course, there are not only advantages but also many disadvantages.

For instance, many who train in their own style find it difficult to reach a certain level of strength.

The forms practiced in formal schools are incorporated precisely because they represent the best and most effective movements.

Simply not knowing them makes it difficult to survive in battle.

That said, there are many, like Rei, who effortlessly leap over—or even shatter—that barrier.

In any case, self-taught styles have their own advantages and disadvantages, just as formal schools do. …Of course, the benefits of formal schools are greater.

The fact that Rei was overwhelming Brewitt—who appeared to be trained in some formal school—without letting a single blow land demonstrated just how extraordinary he was.

“Look, you’re focusing too much on your attacks and neglecting your defense.”

Saying that, Rei lightly thrusts his fist forward, timing it to counter Brewitt’s attack.

That strike wasn’t particularly powerful, and given Brewitt’s resilience, it wouldn’t be difficult for him to withstand.

However, to Brewitt, it seemed as though Rei was intentionally holding back to avoid causing damage, yet attacking in a way that exploited the gaps in Brewitt’s defense.

It felt as though Ray was demonstrating—not with words, but with his actions—just how little attention Blueitt was paying to his defense.

Precisely because Brewitt understood this, Brewitt felt all the more inadequate.

Still, Brewitt understands that Rei is simply on a different level of ability, so he gets back to his feet.

...And yet, rather than focusing on defense as Rei had pointed out, he concentrates even more on his attack—which is typical of Brewitt.

That said, the fact remains that even if Brewitt were to focus a little more on defense right now against a superior opponent like Rei, it would be impossible for him to land a single blow in return.

Even though Rei is clearly a class above Brewitt, it’s only natural for him to want to land a blow on Rei rather than just keep getting beaten one-sidedly.

But Rei, of course, has read Brewitt’s competitive nature.

After all, he’s known Brewitt for a long time… well, not exactly, but they’ve fought side by side on occasion, so their bond is clearly deeper than that with any other noble.

Furthermore, since Rei and Brewitt got along well, Rei has a pretty good grasp of Brewitt’s personality.

That is precisely why Rei fully understood that even if Brewitt were called out on a defensive lapse, he would focus his attention on attacking rather than addressing it.

“Haaah!”

With a sharp exhalation, it was not Brewitt’s fist but his foot that flew toward Rei.

A kick aimed at Rei’s head.

It is generally said that the power of a leg is nearly three times that of an arm.

And for some people, it wouldn’t be surprising if it were even more than that.

For that reason alone, the power of a kick from a giant like Brewitt is undoubtedly top-notch; if it connects, it could knock most opponents unconscious with a single blow.

In some cases, it might even inflict serious damage, such as broken bones.

But… that’s only under normal circumstances; when it comes to Rei facing Brewitt, it’s a different story.

He dodged the kick aimed at his head by simply crouching... and in the next instant, Rei’s eyes widened in surprise.

Because the kick that was supposed to have been aimed at his head had followed him as he crouched.

Still, his surprise lasted only a moment; Rei immediately realized how such a feat had been achieved.

Brewitt’s misfortune was that Rei had repeatedly sparred with Vihera, a female martial arts master.

During those sparring sessions, he had seen Brewitt forcefully alter the trajectory of a kick by using her knee joint—just as Brewitt had just done—on several occasions.

That was precisely why he was able to react instantly to Brewitt’s current kick.

While still crouched, he forcefully pushed off the ground and leaped sideways.

A split second after Rei jumped, Brewitt’s kick swept past the spot where Rei’s body had been.

“Damn, he dodged that too? And that was my best move.”

Brewitt muttered discontentedly as Rei stood up from his crouched position.

True to his words, that mid-air, trajectory-changing kick must have been Brewitt’s trump card.

Since Rei had dodged it on his first try, Brewitt must have been livid.

“Even if it’s a secret move for Brewitt, it’s probably just a standard move for others, right? That’s exactly what I mean.”

Whether he understood what Rei was getting at or not, Brewitt’s expression turned somewhat awkward.

That his secret move was, in fact, just a standard technique. It was only natural, given what he’d been told.

If Rei had been knowledgeable about martial arts while he was in Japan, he might have realized that Brewitt’s kick resembled what’s known as a “Brazilian kick”… but unfortunately, Rei wasn’t that interested in martial arts, so he’d only ever encountered that type of kick for the first time with Vihera’s.

Or maybe he’d seen it in a manga or something… but unfortunately, Rei didn’t remember that.

“I see… Damn it, I thought that one was pretty close. Guess I needed to refine it a bit more.”

“Not really. You were able to dodge that because it’s you, Rei, but it would be difficult for someone seeing it for the first time.”

It wasn’t Rei who responded to Brewitt’s words… but Elena.

Brewitt couldn’t hide his surprise at hearing a third voice suddenly speak up in a place he thought was just him and Rei.

But unlike Brewitt, Rei had noticed Elena’s arrival during the mock battle, so he spoke without seeming particularly surprised.

“That’s true. You could say that Brewitt’s kick lacks both the speed and the sharpness of Vihera’s. Still, it would be difficult to dodge if you’d never seen it before.”

The kick, which seemed aimed at the thigh, changes its trajectory mid-flight to target the torso or head.

Someone who didn’t know any better wouldn’t be able to block it that easily.

“Also, I noticed a slight awkwardness in the movement leading up to the kick. You should work on fixing that too. From what I saw, Brewitt usually attacks with his fists, so it looked like he wasn’t quite used to kicking yet.”

“Guh…”

Brewitt, who was being criticized one after another by Rei and Elena, had no way to argue back.

The fact that he found much of their explanation convincing was likely the reason for his silence.

It was true that he usually relied on punches for his attacks, and there was no doubt that he wasn’t all that accustomed to a fighting style that emphasized kicks.

Seeing Brewitt looking dejected, Rei prefaced his words with “I don’t mean to comfort you, but…” before speaking.

“The fact that you’re still inexperienced right now means, conversely, that there’s still room for growth. If you think of it that way, maybe you don’t need to feel so frustrated?”

Rei’s words must have been unexpected for Brewitt as well.

For a moment, he looked taken aback... but the next instant, a broad smile spread across his face.

“Now that you mention it, you’re right. That means I still have plenty of potential to get stronger. …I see.”

Saying that, Brewitt began experimenting with how to adjust the trajectory of his kick, or how to make the movements leading into the kick flow more smoothly, as if he’d come to terms with it.

Glancing sideways at Brewitt, Rei turned his gaze toward Elena and spoke, looking slightly nervous.

“So? How did it go?”

He didn’t use specific terms like “mediator with the Black Wolf,” asking in a vague manner.

But for Elena, that was enough to understand exactly what Rei wanted to know.

She gave a small but firm nod in response.

“It took a little effort, but I managed to get the information without any problems. Don’t worry.”

“I see. …Well then.”

In response to Elena’s words, Rei called out to Brewitt, who still seemed to be experimenting with various things.

“Brewitt, sorry, but Elena and I have something we need to take care of. Is it okay to call it a day on the mock battle?”

“Huh? Ah… well, I guess it can’t be helped. If I interrupt now, I’ll probably get scolded later.”

Brewitt gave a mischievous—no, a bit of a brat-like—smile, but it was clear he didn’t truly mean it from the bottom of his heart.

Ray suspected there must be some reason Blueitt couldn’t tell him, but he figured Blueitt probably couldn’t bring himself to say it outright, which was why he acted that way.

Naturally, Rei had picked up on Brewitt’s true intentions.

After all, Brewitt had seen him feeling down over the Black Wolf incident, so it was a bit of a stretch to assume that Elena’s arrival meant something romantic was happening.

That said, only Rei could have understood that… Elena, however, took Brewitt’s words at face value, misinterpreted them, and blushed faintly.

Still, the fact that she didn’t retort to Brewitt’s words suggests that Elena, too, didn’t want to make a fuss over nothing.

“Well then, I’ll stay here and train a little more. Oh, and if you could lend me a knight or someone, that would be a big help.”

“Do as you please.”

To begin with, even though they belonged to the same faction, the Brewitt family was a Count’s house, and there was no way they could hold their own against the Duke of Kerebel’s family.

But the fact that he can take this attitude even knowing that is precisely why he is Brewitt.

And there’s no doubt that the fact he’s an acquaintance of Rei, a guest of the Duke of Kerebel’s household, is also having an influence.

...Despite Brewitt’s attitude, Elena felt a mix of bitterness and, at the same time, a certain sense of satisfaction.
