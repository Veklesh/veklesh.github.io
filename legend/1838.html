After running into Brewitt, Rei decided to cancel his plans to go to the library and have tea with Brewitt instead.

To be precise, it was more of a light meal than a simple tea party.

Normally, since the party was set to begin in just a few hours, it’s common practice not to eat much beforehand in preparation for the meal there.

But such conventions didn’t apply to Rei and Brewitt; they were enjoying the sandwiches Miranda had prepared, as well as several dishes stored in Rei’s Misty Ring.

“This soup is delicious. Did you buy it at Anesis?”

“No, it’s from Gilm.”

“……I see.”

As he ate the soup, which was packed with orc meat and vegetables, Rei shook his head in response to Brewitt’s question.

From Brewitt’s perspective, he was probably thinking that if this soup was available in Anesis, he’d like to visit the shop.

Unfortunately, since Rei had bought the soup at a diner in Gilm, Brewitt wouldn’t be able to fully enjoy it.

(Though if he came to Gilm, that would be a different story.)

Thinking that to himself, Rei continued his conversation with Brewitt.

“Come to think of it, among the nobles gathering here today, there’s one who’s hired a former adventurer as a bodyguard. Did you know that?”

“Don’t you think asking me about nobles is a mistake to begin with?”

“……Is that so? Well, now that you mention it, you might be right.”

Brewitt pondered Rei’s words for a moment, but quickly came to agree that he was right.

“Anyway, there are guys like that.”

“Huh. So, what do you want me to do about that? Honestly, unless I know that adventurer personally, I don’t see any point in meeting them.”

“Is that so? Aren’t you both adventurers?”

“Well then, let me ask you this: do you feel a sense of kinship with all nobles, Brewitt?”

Faced with that question, Brewitt was at a loss for words.

If you consider Brewitt purely as a noble, you could say he’s an outlier.

Precisely because of that, when asked if he felt a sense of kinship toward other nobles, as Rei had suggested, he couldn’t simply nod in agreement.

“It was my fault.”

That’s why Brewitt apologized so honestly.

“Nah, it’s not really something that calls for such a formal apology. Besides, I can’t rule out the possibility that that adventurer might actually be an acquaintance of mine.”

All things considered, since Rei arrived in this world of Elgin, he’s interacted with quite a few adventurers.

It’s certainly not impossible that one of those people—just maybe, and I mean just maybe—might actually be serving a noble.

That said, considering the number of adventurers in Elgin—no, in the Kingdom of Mireana—it’s unlikely he’d run into an acquaintance by such a coincidence.

“So, is there anything else to talk about?”

“Well, I don’t really know. I’m not exactly an expert on the nobility or anything. Ah, but… I saw Gaiska’s father.”

“...Gaiska’s, huh?”

At Brewitt’s words, Rei unconsciously wore a look of distaste.

To Rei, Gaiska was definitely not the kind of person he would willingly go out of his way to approach.

He suspected that Gaiska was likely the one who hired the Black Wolf, but he had no proof of that.

So even when told that Gaiska’s father had arrived, Rei couldn’t help but feel uneasy.

However, Brewitt smiled at Rei’s reaction and spoke up.

“Let me tell you, Marquis Seisol is a different breed from Gaiska. You could even say he’s in a league of his own.”

“……Is that so?”

“Yes. To begin with, the House of Seysol wields significant influence even among the Noble Faction. You might think you understand based on Rei alone, but you can’t lump Rei and the Marquis of Seysol together.”

The expression on Brewitt’s face as he said this was not one of jest, but of seriousness.

Faced with such a look, Rei couldn’t just brush it off as a joke.

“Is it really that different?”

“Yeah. I’ve heard that the Seisole Marquis family has grown considerably more powerful under the current head.”

“For example, even among those holding the same title, differences inevitably arise based on the power of the lands the noble owns.”

Beyond that, differences in power arise from various factors, such as the actual abilities of the head of the family and their children.

Rei nodded, assuming that was what Brewitt meant.

“Come to think of it, I’ve heard that too. I’ve heard that a few generations ago, the House of Seysol was on the brink of losing its marquisate. The successive heads of the family managed to gradually build up their power, but under the current head, their influence has surged dramatically.”

Miranda, too, was the daughter of a noble family, even if it was only a baron’s house.

She fully understands just how difficult it is for a noble family to strengthen its position.

That is precisely why her tone carries both deep respect and a sense of awe.

To expand one’s influence to that extent in just a single generation—that simply cannot be achieved through legitimate means alone.

Given that, it’s only natural to assume they’ve employed some shady methods, and it’s precisely because she knows this that she feels that sense of awe.

Then again, the fact that it is awe rather than fear might be proof that Miranda is indeed a noble.

“Is she really that amazing? …If she’s the child of such an amazing person, why did Gaiska turn out the way he did?”

“Well…”

Miranda hesitated, but Brewitt paid no heed to her hesitation and spoke up.

“It’s because he didn’t bother with things like training himself and just lived however he pleased. …Well, when it comes to socializing with the other nobles, he seems to handle it reasonably well.”

There was a hint of jealousy in Brewitt’s expression as he said this.

From Rei’s perspective, there seemed to be absolutely no reason for Brewitt to be jealous of Gaiska… yet the fact that he was showing it on his face like this meant he must have had various thoughts on the matter.

(One possibility is how they interact with other nobles. At the very least, Brewitt isn’t the type who can casually mingle with ordinary nobles. …Even though our personalities mesh well, that much is obvious.)

Rei also understands that he doesn’t get along well with ordinary nobles.

Since he can talk to someone like me so amicably, it was only natural to expect that Brewitt wouldn’t get along with ordinary nobles either.

“Oh, come to think of it…”

Perhaps sensing something in Rei’s gaze, Brewitt changes the subject.

“What happened with that incident during the mock battle the other day?”

“The mock battle… Oh, you mean that incident with the guys who attacked us? Nothing much has come of it. Apparently, they were already in an altered state from some kind of drug, so we couldn’t get any information out of them.”

To be precise, Black Wolf—who likely orchestrated that incident—showed up yesterday… but I didn’t want to drag Brewitt into the Black Wolf affair.

And I don’t want to drag Miranda into it either.

That said, Brewitt aside, Miranda works here at the Duke of Kerebel’s mansion.

If that’s the case, it wouldn’t be strange for her to know that Black Wolf broke into the mansion last night.

(But judging by the looks of things, she probably doesn’t know.)

Perhaps Leodanis, who is close with Miranda, might know the details… but even Leodanis wouldn’t want to drag Miranda into this.

Rei could sort of understand that she wasn’t the type to blab secrets she’d learned on the job to just anyone.

Rei was thinking along those lines when Brewitt suddenly changed the subject.

Well, strictly speaking, he didn’t exactly change the subject, but to someone in the dark, it must have seemed that way.

“Speaking of which, I heard someone broke into this mansion yesterday? I never would have thought there was a daredevil who’d try to break into the Duke of Kerebel’s residence, but I’m surprised they actually succeeded.”

For a moment—just a split second—Ray wondered if Brewitt might be referring to Count Caseres, who had attacked Set, but when he looked into Brewitt’s eyes, he realized he wasn’t talking about that at all… but rather about the Black Wolf.

That said, Brewitt didn’t seem to realize that the intruder was the Black Wolf; he simply seemed to think it was someone else.

“I suppose so. Well, the Duke of Kerebel’s household has its own issues, after all. They were having a party, too.”

“Ah, the servants. …Come to think of it, maybe it can’t be helped. Still, even considering that, I think the intruder must be quite skilled. You think so too, don’t you?”

Brewitt’s gaze turned to Miranda.

Miranda was assigned to Rei, but she showed no sign of disagreeing with Brewitt’s words and simply nodded in agreement.

From Miranda’s perspective, the fact that security at the Duke of Kerebel’s mansion was tight was simply a matter of course—something you could even call common sense.

And yet, the fact that an unknown intruder had managed to get in still felt eerie.

“That’s true. I do hope they catch the intruder as soon as possible. …Though I suppose that won’t be easy.”

Seeing her mutter thoughtfully, Brewitt nodded back, seeming to agree.

“I suppose so. Logically speaking, I can’t imagine there are many people capable of sneaking into a place with such tight security. If that’s the case, then their motive must be…”

His gaze fell on Rei, and even without finishing his sentence, both Rei and Miranda understood what Brewitt was getting at.

“I won’t deny that, but if that’s the case, there’s a possibility that while I might suffer harm, the residents of Kerebel’s mansion won’t.”

“...Is that so? It’s not exactly rare for someone’s skills to not match their personality, you know?”

“Damn,” he thought.

Rei concluded that Brewitt’s words were something he could only say because he had seen the Black Wolf with his own eyes.

That said, since the Black Wolf was manipulating those under the drug’s influence to attack Rei, Set, and Brewitt, the idea that it wouldn’t harm anyone other than its targets was largely just Ray’s own assumption.

Even so, given the current situation, he could still easily cover it up.

“Of course, I wouldn’t say it’s absolutely certain, but based on my experience so far, that’s generally how it goes.”

“Is that how it is?”

Brewitt said that, but Rei couldn’t tell if he was actually convinced by those words.

In any case, since he’d managed to cover it up for the time being, he decided to accept that.

“Yeah. Of course, there are some who are skilled but have the worst personalities, so I wouldn’t say that’s always the case.”

In fact, there’s no correlation between a person’s personality and their skill.

There are those who are incredibly strong despite having a terrible personality, and many who are weak despite having a good one.

After all, even Rei himself couldn’t exactly be called a nice person.

At the very least, a person with a good personality wouldn’t go hunting bandits just to make the loot they’ve stolen their own, nor would they do things like selling captured bandits into slavery.

“I suppose so. If everyone with skill had a good personality, wars and such would have disappeared long ago.”

Brewitt said this, but Rei couldn’t fully agree.

After all, the argument was based on the premise that skilled individuals must also have good character.

However, those who command military forces—such as kings, emperors, or their equivalents—are not necessarily strong themselves.

In Brewitt’s view, this is tantamount to saying that war will continue as long as the nation’s leaders—kings, emperors, and the like—are not strong.

Given that, it implies that war will never end.

(I’ve never met the king of the Mireana Kingdom, but the emperor of the Bestir Empire seemed like the type who would readily seek battle. …That said, there’s no sign of the Bestir Empire attacking right now.)

From Rei’s perspective, as long as those at the top of the country don’t make strange decisions, there shouldn’t be any particular problems.

However, conversely, that implies that if they do make strange decisions, there will be a problem.

Considering the circumstances, I find myself truly hoping that, regardless of whether they are strong or not, they will at least be sharp-witted.
