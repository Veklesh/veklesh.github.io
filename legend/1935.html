“Oh, scales, huh? If they’re from a Giganto Turtle, aren’t they pretty rare?”

As usual, while we were eating dinner in Marina’s backyard and talking about the day’s events, Elena asked this with great interest.

Actually, it wasn’t just Elena who seemed intrigued; everyone else—except for Vihera and Byune, who were actually looking at the scales with their own eyes—felt the same way.

“You’re right. I suppose they are rare. …That said, to really understand what kind of material they are, we’ll need to do some research.”

“I suppose so. …But honestly, I was more surprised to learn there were people pulling the strings behind the Red Cloth.”

Marina was also interested in the Giganto Turtle’s scales, but as a former guild master, she was likely more intrigued by the Red Cloth and the figures behind it.

The kobolds themselves aren’t a threat to the adventurers in Gilm, but that doesn’t mean everyone in Gilm can handle them.

While there are many former adventurers in Gilm—such as those participating in the dismantling of the Giganto Turtle—not everyone is like that.

“As for that, we’re waiting for word from the guards. They should be keeping watch on the base Grange mentioned, so if there’s any movement, we’re set up to receive a report through the guild.”

“It must be tough out there in this cold.”

Ara says this as she looks up at the sky.

Snow is falling where she’s looking, but none of it drifts into Marina’s yard.

This clearly demonstrates just how convenient the spirit magic Marina uses really is.

“Well, as long as I’m here, even if I see the snow falling, I can just think, ‘How beautiful.’”

Vihera, following Ara’s gaze, murmurs with a hint of amusement.

In fact, the current situation—where the snow shows no sign of entering the garden—was just like watching snowfall outside a window from inside the house.

……No, while you can only see outside through a window when you’re inside a house, in Marina’s garden, the view isn’t blocked by walls, so in terms of enjoying the snowy scenery, this is definitely the better option.

“That’s true. Still, since the plan was to keep watch from a nearby building rather than standing outside in the snow, I don’t think we need to worry too much about staying warm.”

Compared to keeping watch outside, even a makeshift shack would shield us from the cold wind, so it’s definitely much easier.

“Hmm. …Still, having to stay completely still is a bit of a drag. I guess it can’t be helped since we’re on watch.”

Many of those listening nodded in agreement at Vihera’s muttered words.

Among those present, there were certainly some who liked or were good at sitting still... but even so, no one would choose to do it willingly.

Of course, the same goes for the guards.

They do it precisely because it’s their job.

(Standing guard... Back in Japan, I’d have manga to kill time... Ah, but if I focus on the manga, might I miss people coming in and out of the building I’m guarding?)

That said, it’s true that just sitting there staring at a spot where nothing is happening is painful.

That’s probably why they keep watch in pairs or trios, chatting about trivial things.

“Anyway, it feels like things have really started moving since the Giganto Turtle incident.”

“Oh, if you’re going to say that, isn’t it more accurate to say it’s been that way since Elena and the others returned from Anesis?”

“Is that so? …Now that you mention it, I suppose you’re right. Though, there were certainly plenty of commotions in Anesis itself.”

As she said this, Elena’s gaze turned toward Rei, who was eating oven-roasted Orc meat.

It was a look that seemed to suggest Rei was attracting trouble like a magnet.

...Rei tried to retort, but when he thought about everything he’d actually experienced so far, he realized he couldn’t deny it—even if someone said he was attracting trouble.

In reality, a large part of it was that Rei had unwittingly gotten himself mixed up in trouble.

Above all, if Rei hadn’t gotten caught up in trouble in Anesis, there’s a high probability that some unknown monster or something would have been summoned right there in Anesis, the second-largest city of the Kingdom of Mireana.

It’s not entirely untrue to say that the reason that didn’t happen was because Rei got caught up in that trouble…

“Ah… but the Kobold incident happened before I even returned to Gilm, right? So isn’t it a bit of a stretch to say I had anything to do with it?”

“That might be true. But it’s an undeniable fact that Rei’s creation of that earthen wall ended up influencing the Red Cloth and the people behind them, isn’t it?”

“I can’t deny that.”

“Oh, I’m not blaming Rei or anything. In fact, thanks to that earthen wall, the number of Kobolds entering Gilm definitely went down. As someone who helped out, I’m happy about that.”

As Marina said this, a radiant smile spread across her face, and Rei smiled back in response.

“Um, ahem. …For now, anyway. We should probably give some serious thought to what the people behind the red cloth might do next, shouldn’t we?”

After clearing her throat as if to cover something up, Elena spoke, and Vihera nodded in agreement.

“That’s true. Even though the guards are watching, there’s a chance they might notice… and if that happens, they might strike first.”

“Strike first, huh. If that happens, it’ll actually make things easier for us. …And I’m sure it’d be easier on Set, too.”

Watching Set and Yellow running around the garden in the mild, spring- or autumn-like temperatures despite it being a winter night, Rei murmurs.

“There’s no doubt that would be easier. But… if they get flustered, they might resort to some tactic we never even considered. If that happens, the residents of Gilm will suffer.”

“……If it doesn’t cause any harm to Gilm, then powerful monsters would actually be more than welcome. Rei, don’t forget about our promise for tomorrow.”

“Promise?” Hearing Vihera’s words, the others—except for Byune, who was focused on her meal—turned their gaze toward Rei.

That said, this “promise” wasn’t anything as suggestive as the others might be thinking.

The Snow Cyclops that Rei defeated this morning.

Rei and Vihera had made a promise that if such a powerful monster appeared tomorrow morning, she would be the one to fight it.

For Vihera, who loved fighting strong opponents more than anything, missing out on such a golden opportunity was simply not an option.

“I know I’m repeating myself, but there’s no guarantee there’ll be a powerful monster like the one Vihera’s expecting, okay? If tomorrow turns out to have only weak monsters like Kobolds or goblins left, don’t complain.”

Even as he said that, Rei himself was hoping that a powerful, unknown monster would make an appearance.

That’s precisely why he didn’t go so far as to use terrain manipulation to replace the blood-soaked ground left over from the Giganto Turtle’s dismemberment with soil from underground.

“I know that. But I think it’ll probably be fine.”

“……What makes you think that?”

“A woman’s intuition.”

Her tone was full of confidence, and seeing that, Rei almost found himself convinced.

But the fact was, when it came to “a woman’s intuition,” Rei, being a man, had no way to argue against it.

And in fact, he knew from his experiences since coming to this world that a woman’s intuition was sharper than he’d ever expected.

Above all, Vihera had relied on that intuition countless times in battle to seize decisive opportunities.

Given that, there was no way Rei would ever dare to underestimate that intuition.

(Even in manga, there are depictions where a woman’s intuition is portrayed as being almost superhumanly sharp… but Vihera is the real deal.)

In Japan, Rei had never really felt the sharpness of a woman’s intuition, but since coming to this world, he had witnessed such things time and time again.

That’s exactly why he never questioned Vihera’s words.

“...Yeah, Vihera’s intuition really is amazing.”

Early the next morning, Rei muttered to himself as he held the Death Scythe and the Twilight Spear.

As he dodged the attacks of the monsters before him—giant, white-haired apes nearly two meters tall, and multiple at that—Rei felt once again that Vihera’s woman’s intuition was not to be underestimated.

Parrying the fists—which possessed enough power to easily snap a tree growing right there—with the Twilight Spear, he swung the Death Scythe to slice the creature’s torso in two.

The white monkey was killed in an instant... As he defeated the snow monkeys, Rei looked at Vihera, who was fighting with a look of joy on her face.

The way she evaded the snow monkey’s attacks with claws conjured from magic while slashing through its skin seemed more like dancing than fighting.

It wasn’t just Rei who was captivated by Vihera’s battle—many others were as well… specifically, most of those who had come for the butchering.

Those who work as adventurers and are reasonably accustomed to combat are captivated by Vihera’s efficient fighting style, wondering how one could move like that.

And those with almost no combat experience, like Suchi, are simply captivated by the sheer beauty of Vihera’s dance-like movements.

“Grrrrrrrrrrr!”

A short distance away, Set is using Power Crush to shatter the Snow Ape’s skull.

The power of the skill, now at Level 6, does more than just shatter the snow monkey’s skull; with a single blow, it reduces the flesh, bones, and brain matter to an unbelievably fine consistency—scattering them around as if they’d been pulverized by a specialized machine.

...Rei understood just how devastating the attack was, but if even the right ear ended up like that, it would be unusable as a proof-of-kill part.

That said, Rei wasn’t exactly short on money, so losing a proof of kill part or two wouldn’t be a big deal.

If even the most important magic stone had been crushed, though, he might have gotten angry at Set.

“Well, I’ll take one, and Set will take one. …In that case, I’d better leave the rest to Vihera.”

Muttering to himself, he stowed the Death Scythe and the Twilight Spear in his Misty Ring, and did the same with the Snow Ape’s corpse.

“Grr.”

Set, who had crushed the head, dragged the snow monkey’s corpse over to Rei with his beak and let out a growl.

Rei stowed away the headless snow monkey’s corpse while patting Set on the head and praising him for a job well done.

Set, his head being stroked, narrowed his eyes in apparent delight and comfort.

As they shared this moment, Rei watched the battle at Vihera alongside Set.

“Hmm.”

When she turned her gaze toward the voice that had suddenly caught her attention, she saw Byune standing there.

It was certainly not just Rei’s imagination that he sensed a hint of envy in the way she was watching Vihera.

“What’s wrong? Do you want to fight the Snow Monkeys with Vihera too, Byune?”

In response to Rei’s question, Byune nodded, her expression as impassive as ever.

That said, Rei understood that this wasn’t because she was seeking combat, but because she was interested in the parts of the Snow Ape that could be sold for money—such as materials, magic stones, and the parts required for the subjugation certificate.

But honestly, it’s doubtful that Byune, in her current state, could actually fight a Snow Ape.

(She might be able to handle one on her own, but the Snow Ape’s true strength seems to lie in its coordinated attacks. …Come to think of it, it was probably just luck that Set and I were able to take down one of them.)

In the distance, a single Snow Ape was attacking Vihera with desperate ferocity.

The sharp claws extending from its fingers would, under normal circumstances, easily pierce skin, tear flesh, and shatter bones.

But that’s only true if they land—no matter how powerful an attack is, it’s meaningless if it doesn’t hit.

On the contrary, Vihera dodged the snow monkey’s attacks while slashing at its body with the claws on her gauntlets and the blades extending from the heels of her greaves.

Its white fur, true to its name “Snow Ape,” is dyed red by its own blood.

The Snow Ape tries to capitalize on the opening created by its comrades, but even so, Vihera evades its movements and slices through the Snow Ape’s body with a series of dance-like strikes.

And so, in the end... the Snow Ape lay lifeless on the snow-covered ground.
