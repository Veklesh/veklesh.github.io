As expected—or perhaps I should say, as was to be expected—Liberte was furious upon hearing that someone had broken into her mansion and, worse still, attempted to attack Set, the familiar of Rei (a man) whom she was treating as a guest.

She likely concluded that attacking the familiar of her guest, Rei, was tantamount to showing contempt for the Duke of Kerebel’s household.

As a result, she subjected the captives to a harsh interrogation, even using magic items...

“Nonsense! Why would you do such a thing! …Even as a joke, this isn’t funny.”

Standing before the messenger from Liberte, the man—Count Caseres, who wielded considerable influence even within the Noble Faction—spoke those words with a forced smile.

The letter the messenger had brought stated that Count Caseres was to be expelled from the Noble Faction and would henceforth be forbidden from calling himself a member of the Noble Faction.

“I’m afraid I can’t tell you. I’m merely the one who brought this letter from Liberte.”

“...In that case, I will go and speak with Kerebel Duke directly to clear up this misunderstanding!”

If he were to be expelled from the Noble Faction like this, it would spell his ruin.

The nobles of the Noble Faction would look down on me with contempt, leaving me with only two options: join the Royal Faction or remain unaffiliated with any of the three major factions.

As for the Neutral Faction, having attacked Set, the familiar of Rei—Daskar’s right-hand man—there’s no way they would accept him.

Admittedly, Count Caseres still had the option of exile, but if he were to do such a thing, it was doubtful he could survive as a noble, and above all, he had no connections abroad.

Given his situation, he had to find a way to avoid expulsion from the Noble Faction at all costs.

(Damn it, Duke Kerebel… He should value me more than some upstart from the Neutral Faction!)

A strong sense of frustration simmered within the Earl of Cáceres, but with the Duke of Kerebel’s envoy standing right before him, he could not possibly voice it.

In fact, if asked who was at fault in this whole affair, the Earl of Cáceres would immediately declare it was Rei.

During the mock battle, he went out of his way to flaunt a griffin for all to see.

It is simply unacceptable for a mere adventurer to lay claim to such a rare monster.

If that were the case, that griffin would surely have been happier as his own.

……Given that he views Set as a possession, it was probably fortunate for Count Caseres that Rei never found out.

In any case, he had been making various preparations ever since he saw Set in the mock battle. Upon hearing about the party being held tonight to reward the servants, he put his plan into action… but it all ended up backfiring.

Count Caseres hadn’t expected Kerebel’s side to find out about his actions, but he hadn’t anticipated them forcing the situation to this extent.

And above all, the fatal misunderstanding was… that Duke Kerebel had prioritized Rei—who was merely an adventurer—over Count Caseres.

If he had clearly prioritized the Count of Cáceres, he naturally wouldn’t have resorted to measures like expulsion from the Noble Faction.

Even if it hadn’t gone that far, if he’d cared even a little, he would have waited a while… specifically, until the Count of Cáceres had a chance to cover up any evidence—and only then would he have sent word. Of course, that message wouldn’t have been about expulsion from the Noble Faction, but rather phrased as a casual inquiry asking if he had any idea what was going on.

“Damn it!”

“...What is it?”

The messenger from Liberte tilted his head at Count Cáceres, who had inadvertently let his frustration show.

Of course, it was merely an act.

In reality, the messenger knew exactly what the Count of Cáceres was trying to say.

That he had made a fatal mistake due to his own lack of foresight.

If this had been done not by the Count of Caseres himself, but by one of his relatives, there might still have been a way to salvage the situation.

But since it was the head of the Caseres family himself who did it, there was no excuse.

From the messenger’s perspective, the question remains: why did the head of the household himself do such a foolish thing?

If he was going to do it anyway, he should have used someone he could cut loose as a middleman, the messenger thought.

But in the end, the man was merely a messenger for Liberte, and there was no need for him to offer unsolicited advice.

Perhaps the Count of Cáceres understood this as well; though the flesh on his sagging cheeks twitched in displeasure, he said nothing more. …No, he couldn’t say anything.

If he were to say anything here, it could very well lead to the worst possible outcome for him, so that’s only natural.

“It’s nothing. But… isn’t the content of this letter a bit too exaggerated? If possible, I’d like Duke Kerebel to be a little more level-headed.”

“Master is calm. No, I might even say he is too kind—perhaps even excessively so.”

“What did you say?”

Thinking his master had been insulted, the man replied, his mood souring.

Count Caseres replied with a sour expression, but in reality, if Liberte truly considered Count Caseres someone he could never forgive, he would have come here in person leading his men, rather than sending a letter.

But it seemed Caseres hadn’t considered that possibility.

He shot the messenger a displeased look.

Perhaps realizing he didn’t want to speak with Count Caseres any further, the messenger made no attempt to continue the conversation and simply bowed.

“Well then, I’ll take my leave now.”

“Hey, wait! Is what’s written in this letter true?! I demand an explanation from Duke Kerebel!”

“I’m sorry, but I’m only here to deliver the letter; I have no authority to do so.”

Saying this, the messenger bowed and left the room.

Count Caseres glared at the man’s retreating back with utter contempt.

“Damn it! Who do you think I am!? I’ve gone through so much trouble and lent my support to the Noble Faction all this time… and now you’re just going to cut me loose over something like this!?”

As he spoke, his frustration seemed to grow. Count Cáceres slammed his fist down on his desk in a fit of irritation.

...Though, the very next moment, he found himself groaning from the pain of the blow.

For Count Caseres, however, that pain served as a catalyst to calm his mind—which had been so overwhelmed by anger and agitation that he could no longer think straight—allowing him to regain some composure.

“What should I do? If I’m expelled from the Noble Faction under these circumstances, the future looks hopeless. Should I switch sides to the Royal Faction? But… Count Helkena is on their side. I absolutely refuse to serve under that man.”

Count Herkena, who ruled the territory adjacent to that of Count Caseres.

His family has been at odds with the Count of Caseres for generations, and even now, they are locked in a bitter dispute over water rights.

Serving under such a man is something the Count of Cáceres could never bear.

“In that case… should I operate without belonging to any faction? Are you telling me to become a loser like that?”

Among the nobles of the Kingdom of Mireana, there are a fair number who do not belong to any of the three major factions.

While many of them act this way based on their own convictions, in the eyes of the Count of Cáceres, such people are nothing more than losers even among the nobility.

As the head of the proud House of Caseres, he absolutely refused to become such a loser.

But, of course, now that word had gotten out that he’d laid a hand on Set, he couldn’t join the Neutral Faction either…

“What the hell am I supposed to do?!”

Once again, the Earl of Cáceres slammed his fist down on his desk in frustration.

...The Earl of Cáceres did not know.

That Liberte was concerned about the fact that, even among the Noble Faction, his domain imposed particularly heavy taxes on its subjects.

He had coldly judged that there was a high probability of a riot breaking out in the Earl of Caseres’s domain in the near future, and that keeping the Earl of Caseres in the Noble Faction would deal a significant blow to the faction, so he had cut him loose.

If the one who plotted this attack on Set had been someone other than the Count of Cáceres—someone who, while belonging to the Noble Faction, did not harm the faction—Liberte likely would not have imposed such severe punishment.

They would have imposed a punishment sufficient to satisfy Rei, but it surely wouldn’t have gone as far as expulsion from the Noble Faction.

Count Caseres failed to realize this and simply vented his frustration... The next day, he told his wife and sons about the incident, and ultimately, his wife granted him a divorce and his in-laws disowned him.

“...What do you think? I believe Rei is satisfied.”

It was already past midnight—the sort of hour that, under normal circumstances, would be called the dead of night—when Rei heard those words in Liberte’s office.

Surprised that she was still working after the party had ended, Rei hesitated, unsure how to respond.

Still, considering the current situation...

“If Duke Kerebel has decided that punishment is sufficient, then I have no objection. After all, Set didn’t suffer any serious injuries. However, if the punishment is merely expulsion from the Noble Faction, won’t there be others who come up with similar ideas?”

From Rei’s perspective, he questioned whether being expelled from a faction was really a sufficient punishment.

Although he’s currently part of a party, he’s primarily a solo operator, so he wondered if such a mild punishment would simply lead to more troublemakers like that.

“Hmm, you might not understand this, Rei, since you’re used to going your own way, but for a noble… especially someone like Count Caseres, this incident is a pretty severe punishment. In fact, I’d go so far as to say that as long as you’re in Anesis, no one will dare target Set.”

“Is it really that serious?”

“Yes. Depending on the situation, it wouldn’t be surprising if, even after Rei leaves Anesis because of this incident, the number of people trying to cause trouble decreases.”

Hearing that, Rei had no choice but to accept it for the time being.

If something like this were to happen again, he might have grounds to complain about Liberte’s judgment… but that’s a conversation for when something like this actually happens again.

“I understand. Then I won’t say anything more. But… even if we go that far, if there’s someone who comes after us again…”

Rei said nothing more, but for Liberte, that was enough.

“Hmm. In that case, you may do as you please. Though, as I said earlier, once people see what punishment Count Caseres received for this incident, I doubt anyone would be so reckless as to make a move.”

The reason he couldn’t say there wouldn’t be any at all was likely because there were those among the Noble Faction who believed they could get away with anything.

Exactly the kind of noble who, even after learning of Count Caseres’s fate, might well think, “If it had been me, I could have handled it much better…”

In reality, while the Count of Cáceres did make various blunders, there were also aspects where he handled things quite skillfully.

Among them was someone who, blackmailed over a debt, acted as a guide for the three intruders.

Set had already extracted the details of the situation from the three men he had repelled, and the guard in question had already been apprehended.

If the men’s target had been someone other than Set—someone more ordinary… someone more docile—there is a very real possibility that the Count of Cáceres’s plan would have succeeded.

Furthermore, the fact that he acted before the others is also worthy of praise.

(He’s certainly not incompetent, mind you. …But precisely because he isn’t incompetent, we judged that he would cause greater harm to the Noble Faction, so we decided to cut him loose.)

Even if one possesses capable abilities, it is meaningless if those abilities are used to oppress the common people.

Moreover, he was actively maneuvering within the Noble Faction itself, and there is no doubt that, had things continued this way, serious trouble would have inevitably arisen in the near future.

That is precisely why things turned out this way.

“Anyway, let’s leave the matter of Count Caseres for now. …We’ve never had a chance to talk just the two of us like this before, so would it be okay if we chatted a bit?”

“Huh? Oh, sure. I don’t mind at all.”

Even as he said that, Rei was well aware that his bodyguards were waiting in the next room, and that several others were hiding in the ceiling and under the floor.

Given the situation, it was hardly what one would call “just the two of us”… but to Liberte, the bodyguards probably didn’t count in this instance.

She smiled happily at the thought of hearing from Rei how Elena had spent her time while in Gilm.
