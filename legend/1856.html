"Rei, how about this!"

With a thud, the soup bowl is placed on the table.

Inside the bowl were soup and noodles.

...Yes, even though she should have been busy with the New Year’s party, Georgima was still continuing her ramen research.

While she couldn’t devote herself to it entirely, Rei couldn’t help but be impressed by Georgima’s passion for cooking, which had driven her to study ramen as much as possible.

Especially since she’d managed to produce a bowl of ramen that was actually ready for tasting.

The aroma of fish sauce wafts from the soup, stimulating the appetite.

(How does ramen made with fish sauce fit into the categories? Since it’s fish sauce, is it still okay to call it soy sauce ramen? No, but ramen made with shotturu wasn’t called soy sauce ramen—it was called shotturu ramen.)

For now, let’s just call it ramen, and I turn my gaze back to the soup bowl.

The soup bowl, which is slightly deeper than usual, is probably just the right size for holding the ramen.

Inside the bowl are only the soup and the noodles.

In a typical bowl of ramen, this would be topped with a boiled egg, chashu pork, menma, nori, and chopped green onions, but since the focus here is on the noodles, those kinds of toppings are likely absent.

Picking up the soup bowl in front of him, Rei takes a sip of the soup.

It wouldn’t be strange if people called it rude to drink directly from a bowl… no, a soup bowl.

But since this was a special occasion, Rei simply felt like drinking it that way.

I suppose it wouldn’t be a problem to drink it with a spoon, but the main reason was that it just seemed to taste better this way.

The first thing that spread across his palate was an almost rich, savory fish flavor.

He had drunk soup made with fish sauce by Georgima before, but the flavor of this sip… the depth of it, if you will, was different.

“Delicious.”

At Rei’s single muttered word, Georgima and all his apprentices broke into satisfied smiles.

The New Year’s party, and the staff appreciation party held the day before.

They prepared the dishes for these events without cutting any corners, yet they never stopped their research on ramen prototypes.

Since Rei had said he would be returning to Gilm in just a few days, it was only natural that he would want to make the most of every opportunity.

Since he’d managed to get them to say it was delicious after tasting the soup—which was the culmination of all that hard work—it would be impossible not to feel happy about it.

(It’s a little different from the ramen soup I’m used to… but well, it’s fish sauce instead of soy sauce, so I guess that’s to be expected. If I keep researching, there’s definitely room for it to evolve further. And eventually, I’ll get around to soy sauce and miso too…)

While imagining the future of ramen broth, Rei next wraps the noodles around his fork.

Thinking that chopsticks would be better for this, he brings the noodles to his mouth.

The act of slurping—which is highly valued in ramen culture, yet often considered bad manners in other countries—is impossible when eating ramen in the same way one would eat pasta.

Feeling a slight pang of regret over this, Rei savors the noodles.

(It’s similar, but different.)

That was Rei’s honest impression after tasting the noodles.

Compared to udon, they were clearly closer to the ramen noodles Rei knew.

They were getting closer... but still, the taste and texture of the noodles were somehow different from the ramen noodles Rei knew.

“Delicious.”

When Rei finished eating the noodles and said that, it wasn’t flattery or anything like that. It was an honest, heartfelt sentiment.

But even so... or perhaps precisely because of that, a bitter expression flashed across Georgima’s face as she watched.

From Rei’s expression, she understood that he had indeed said the noodles were delicious, but at the same time, she read that it wasn’t quite what he had expected.

At the same time, it was enough to convince Georgima that Rei had, in fact, eaten ramen before.

Otherwise, he wouldn’t have said it was different from what he expected—rather than simply calling it good or bad—after eating a dish he’d only seen in a book.

(Then why didn’t he say so? …Well, this is Rei we’re talking about. There’s undoubtedly some reason for it. Given Rei’s personality, it’s unlikely he’s just being coy.)

Holding back a slight sense of dissatisfaction, but deciding there were more pressing matters at hand, Georgima spoke up.

“Rei, be honest. Those ramen noodles… aren’t they different from what you expected?”

At Georgima’s words, the apprentices, who had been delighted by Rei’s reaction, showed their surprise.

To the other disciples, it had seemed as though Rei was happily eating the ramen they had made.

This, perhaps, is the major difference between a master and a disciple.

Stared at intently by Georgima, who was giving him a look that seemed to say, “I won’t tolerate a lie,” Rei eventually nodded reluctantly.

“Yeah. To be precise, the texture of these noodles feels different from what I read in the book. But that’s just my impression—maybe…”

“No, it’s not.”

Cutting Rei off, Georgima stated it with certainty.

It was the kind of statement that truly deserved the phrase “leaving no room for argument.”

“Enough with the excuses. Did you feel that these noodles were different from ramen noodles, or not? That’s all I want to hear.”

“...No.”

In response to Georgima’s question—no, it was more like an interrogation—Rei could only manage that single answer.

It was because, looking into Georgima’s eyes, he realized she had seen through him—that he knew what ramen was… not just from books, but that he had actually eaten it.

But that was something shared only between Rei and Georgima; the others around them had no idea.

While the true meaning of their exchange likely remained a mystery to those around them, for Georgima, it was only natural to focus her attention on making the dish she had prepared even more delicious rather than on the people nearby.

“So? How exactly is it different?”

“Well, as I said earlier, the texture… the feel when I chewed the noodles seemed different from the noodles I’ve read about in books. I think I mentioned this before, but ramen noodles are supposed to use something called kansui. I don’t know exactly what that is, but… what did you use in these?”

“It’s a concentrated liquid made from the bodily fluids of a certain monster. Apparently, in a country quite far from here, they use it to make bread.”

At Georgima’s words, Rei’s eyes widened in surprise.

They’d been talking about kansui, so he never in a million years expected to hear about some monster’s bodily fluids or whatever.

“Well… for starters, I don’t think that’s lye.”

At the very least, the brine Rei knew of was the kind commonly used in Japan—no, on Earth—so there was no way to obtain it by concentrating monster bodily fluids.

That said, Rei didn’t have any particular thoughts on the idea of concentrating monster bodily fluids.

To begin with, since monsters were generally viewed as food, Rei’s understanding was that there was no particular problem with treating their bodily fluids as ingredients—though he wasn’t sure if that was the correct term in this case.

Even on Earth, bodily fluids are used in various ways, such as incorporating animal blood into sausages or using shark blood in health supplements.

Rei vaguely remembered that much, though it was a distant memory. …Though, if asked exactly how animal blood is used in cooking, he wouldn’t be able to answer—which is just like Rei.

Given that, even when told that monster bodily fluids were used as a substitute for alkaline water, Rei didn’t find it particularly strange.

……Unfortunately, the result turned out to be slightly different from the noodles Rei was familiar with.

“Is that so? But Rei doesn’t know what ‘alkali water’ actually is, does she? Then, perhaps…”

“Uh… Well, for starters, I don’t think it said to use monster bodily fluids.”

Setting aside the fact that they now knew he’d eaten ramen before, there was no way he could tell them he’d actually come to this world of Elgin from another realm.

(Well, considering Georgima’s personality, even if she heard I came from another world, she probably wouldn’t think much of it? …Though there’s a high chance she’d ask me to teach her how to cook food from another world.)

Given that Georgima has what could only be called an obsession with cooking, it’s practically a certainty.

Unfortunately, even when I was in Japan, Rei wasn’t particularly skilled at cooking.

In fact, even when it comes to dishes like udon, pizza, or meat buns, he can only give a rough explanation; it’s the chefs who actually research and prepare them.

Even if Georgima asked him to teach her about Japanese—or Earth—cuisine, he could explain what the dishes are, but if she asked how to make them, he’d be completely at a loss.

The ramen Georgima is struggling with right now is proof of that.

What kind of dish is ramen… the flavor of the broth, the texture of the noodles, the toppings.

I could explain what those elements are like, but since I don’t actually know how to make it, Georgima and the others are trying their best to make ramen like this.

If this is what happens with just one dish like ramen, then if I were to teach them other dishes that Rei knows... I wouldn’t even know when we’d be able to return to Gilm.

Or there’s even the possibility that Georgima might come to Gilm with Rei.

As long as she’s employed by the Duke of Kerebel’s household, she shouldn’t be able to pull a stunt like that, but considering Georgima’s obsession with cooking, the possibility of her resorting to such measures is by no means low.

No, far from being unlikely—Rei had a strong premonition that they would definitely come to Gilm.

“It’s not bodily fluid, is it? …In that case, should I not use this?”

Saying that, Georgima turned her gaze toward a jar sitting among the seasonings.

From that gesture, Rei understood that the contents of that jar were precisely the concentrated monster bodily fluid used as a substitute for water this time.

Georgima looked slightly disappointed, but Rei put a stop to it.

“Why not just use it? The ramen I ate was definitely a little different from what I expected. But compared to udon, there’s no doubt it’s closer to ramen noodles. So, I think the best course of action is to keep using that fluid to refine the recipe… as long as we don’t know what kansui actually is.”

That’s right. Since they didn’t know exactly what kansui was, Rei felt the best course of action was to continue searching for it while simultaneously refining the noodles made from the monster’s bodily fluid.

Since we aren’t using kansui, the result might not actually be ramen.

But even so, it would likely be a dish similar to ramen, and there’s a very good chance it could even surpass ramen.

Given that, Rei felt it would be a waste to throw away that opportunity.

“Is that so? Well, this ramen… no, maybe we can’t even call it ramen, but I have a feeling it might turn out pretty tasty.”

In response to Rei’s words, Georgima replied, sounding not entirely displeased.

His usually stern face broke into a smile—just a little, just a tiny bit, but it was a happy one.

Normally, it would be hard to believe that Rei, who had never eaten ramen before, would say such a thing.

But Georgima understood that Rei actually had experience eating ramen.

That’s why, despite Rei’s words, she didn’t voice any particular complaints and simply accepted them quietly.

“Alright. I won’t stop looking for kansui, but I’d also like to research ramen using these seasonings.”

“Seasoning?” Rei tilted his head at the word for just a moment, but he quickly realized she was referring to monster bodily fluids.

He was slightly surprised that Georgima would call bodily fluids “seasonings,” but since Rei himself had no qualms about using animal blood as a seasoning—no, as an ingredient—it must stem from his mindset as a chef.

“Yeah, go ahead. If we keep researching using those… seasonings, we might just end up with ramen that transcends ramen itself.”

At Rei’s words, Georgima flashed a smile that could only be described as ferocious—the kind of grin one would never expect to see on a chef’s face.
