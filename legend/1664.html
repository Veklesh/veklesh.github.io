"Yum! What is this? It's so good!"

Hearing those words come from Rei’s mouth, Bistol broke into a broad smile.

“Hehe, how is it? My special seafood soup, made with plenty of love.”

“I don’t really care about Bistol’s love or anything, but this seafood soup is seriously delicious.”

As the name “seafood soup” suggests, the main ingredients are seafood.

These were the seafood Rei and the others had caught today.

It wasn’t just fish; there were plenty of shellfish as well, which added a rich umami flavor to the broth.

What surprised Rei most of all was that the soup was also packed with shellfish that looked exactly like abalone.

Back in Japan, abalone sold at supermarkets often cost over a thousand yen a piece.

And yet, the pot was absolutely packed with them.

Of course, the shellfish in the pot weren’t just abalone. There were shells resembling turban shells, shells similar to the mussels he’d seen on TV, and plenty of other shells Rei had never even seen before.

Of course, it wasn’t just shellfish; there were also large quantities of fish, shrimp, and squid… plus edible wild herbs and mountain vegetables that grew near the hot spring where they were staying. The enormous pot was brimming with a massive amount of seafood soup.

...Or so it was, but the contents of the pot rapidly dwindled as Rei, Byune, and the others ate their fill.

“Anyone who drinks this soup will be corrupted. …Let’s call this the ‘Soup of Corruption.’”

“Um, Marina? I think that’s a bit of an exaggeration…”

Bistol spoke hesitantly, clearly taken aback by the name “Soup of Corruption” that had come out of Marina’s mouth.

Since cooking was one of Bistol’s hobbies, he was happy that someone enjoyed his cooking so much. He was happy, but… even so, he would have preferred to avoid having his dish named “Soup of Decadence.”

“No, I don’t think it’s strange at all to have a name like that. The concentrated seafood essence goes perfectly with bread.”

Not only Marina, but Elena also agreed with the name “Decadent Seafood Soup.”

Vihera seemed to have no objections either, nodding as she ate the soup’s ingredients.

Although this “Decadent Seafood Soup” is classified as a soup, it contains such a large amount of ingredients that its flavor and portion size are substantial enough to be considered a main dish.

That’s precisely why tonight’s dinner consists solely of the “Decadent Seafood Soup” made by Bistol and some bread.

Hearing that a meal consists of nothing but soup and bread, many might think it sounds meager, depending on the situation.

But for Rei and the others actually eating it, the meal was more than satisfying.

“Hehe, I’m so glad you’re enjoying it so much. My cooking is made with lots of love,”

Seeing Bistrel say this with such joy, if one ignored her appearance, it wouldn’t have been wrong to describe her as motherly.

That said, given her muscular build, there’s no doubt she could never be called “motherly.”

“This would probably taste good with udon, too.”

“I suppose so. But since this is a special ‘Decadent Seafood Soup,’ I think thinner udon noodles would be better so they can soak up the broth.”

“Like somen noodles?”

“……Somen?”

At the mention of somen during the conversation with Marina, everyone’s gaze turned toward Rei, just as it had when the topic of sashimi came up.

Realizing exactly what kind of explanation was expected of him, Rei opened his mouth.

“To put it simply, it’s udon that’s much thinner than the udon in Gilm.”

Strictly speaking, udon and somen are similar, but they are different.

However, Rei didn’t have detailed knowledge on the subject; for now, he simply understood that somen was thinner than udon.

“Come to think of it, even among the udon served in Gilm, the thickness varies quite a bit depending on the shop or food stall, doesn’t it?”

Hearing the word “thin udon,” Vihera murmured that.

In fact, she was absolutely right.

Some shops serve thick udon like Sanuki udon, while others serve thin, slightly flat udon like Inaniwa udon.

Naturally, the thickness of the udon is the result of trial and error by each shop to find the perfect match for the dishes they serve.

It varies depending on whether the broth is rich or light, and it also depends on the ingredients in the broth.

“I should have at least asked them to prepare some thin udon noodles.”

As he said this, Rei realized that perhaps ramen, rather than udon, would actually go better with this decadent seafood soup.

Of course, since he had no idea how to make ramen noodles, he had no intention of voicing that thought and causing any unnecessary commotion.

(They use eggs, right? Other than that, just flour, water, and salt? Nah, there’s no point in thinking about that now.)

He quickly banished thoughts of ramen noodles—or rather, Chinese-style noodles—from his mind and returned to savoring the seafood soup.

Once the meal was over, the rest of the time was free.

During that time, Bistol began filleting the fish, soaking it in brine, and making it into dried fish to preserve it.

Bistoll’s magic pouch could hold a lot of items, but it wasn’t like the Misty Ring, where time didn’t pass inside.

Therefore, the fish Bistol put into the magic pouch had to be prepared for preservation rather than left raw.

“Guruu”

“Kyu!”

Beside Bistol, Set and Yellow were staring intently—perhaps intrigued by the sight of him fileting the fish, or simply because the smell of the fish had whetted their appetites.

Yellow was lying on his stomach right on top of Set, who was lying on his stomach on the ground... that was the situation.

If anyone here—whether from Milein’s or Johanna’s group, or even those led by Sura who were devoted to Set—had seen this, they would undoubtedly have been deeply shocked.

“Hehe, here, have some of this. But no sneaking bites, okay?”

Watching the two of them, Bistol smiled slightly as she tossed them a small fish—one so tiny it wouldn’t even be worth selling as dried fish.

Set and Yellow caught it perfectly and began eating it with relish.

...As for why he had secured fish that weren’t fit for sale, it was simply so that Bistol could make some dried fish for his own consumption.

Even if they’re hard to sell, they’re perfectly fine for his own consumption.

When you think of them as a snack to go with sake, homemade dried fish offers the satisfaction of having made them yourself, making them the absolute best.

Besides, since he was making this much dried fish anyway, making a little extra for his own consumption didn’t require much more effort.

That’s why Bistol was turning even the smaller fish into dried fish.

A short distance away from Bisturu, Rei was trying desperately to remember how to make fish sauce… or rather, shotturu.

But the cooking manga Rei relied on when cooking didn’t include instructions for fish sauce… or if it did, she hadn’t remembered them; all she knew was what she’d seen in a news special or something like that.

(Soak the fish in salt? No, did they do that? Then, boil it in salt water? That doesn’t quite sound right either. Since soy sauce is supposed to be a fermented food, maybe they ferment salted fish? …That sounds like it would turn into something pretty intense. Maybe I shouldn’t try making it myself after all—I’ll just have to look around the nearby fishing villages or port towns.)

Of course, whether or not fishing villages or port towns actually have fish sauce is something I won’t know until I go there myself.

In fact, it’s more likely that they don’t have any at all.

If that’s the case, I’ll have to look for other places that might have fish sauce… but as a last resort, Rei will have to make fish sauce on her own.

(Maybe I should just give up on fish sauce? But… Shotturu hot pot is so delicious. Well, there are vegetables similar to green onions, but there’s no tofu, and there aren’t any fish that look like sandfish, so it’d probably just end up being a makeshift version.)

It would have been best if she knew how to make regular soy sauce, but unfortunately, she has absolutely no idea how to make that either—not even as much as she knows about fish sauce.

For Rei, soy sauce was something that was always on the table back in Japan, so she had absolutely no idea how it was actually made.

So, even though my memory of the recipe is a bit fuzzy, I tried to make the fish sauce I did remember...

(Worst case, I’ll just leave it to Gimekara.)

Rei thinks back to Gimekara, who taught him how to make natto, and considers giving up halfway.

It’s a hassle, but if I can actually make fish sauce, there’s no doubt it could become a business.

Of course, it’s possible that fish sauce exists here in Elgin—or even in the Kingdom of Mireana—but I just don’t know about it.

(It has a pretty strong flavor, but I don’t think people are as picky about it as they are with natto… so it should be a decent business opportunity.)

Having thought that far, Rei suddenly realizes something.

“So, the fact that I can’t eat sashimi right away still hasn’t changed, has it?”

“Hm? What’s wrong? I thought you were deep in thought, but then you suddenly…”

Vihera, who had been cleaning her gauntlets a short distance away from Rei, asked.

But Rei shook his head at Vihera, saying it was nothing.

“I was just thinking that even if I were to eat sashimi, I don’t have any condiments.”

“Oh, raw fish… All the dishes Rei knows look delicious, but raw fish is a bit much for me.”

Although Vihera trusted Rei, she seemed a bit disappointed, perhaps because the idea of raw fish still gave her pause.

Since it was a dish Rei recommended, it was undoubtedly delicious.

She knew that, but the common sense she’d lived by all her life wasn’t something she could just shake off so easily.

That’s exactly why she felt a bit disappointed.

“I’m not forcing you, so don’t worry about it. Actually, as far as I know, there are people who can’t stand sashimi.”

It’s not like every Japanese person loves sashimi…

Some Japanese people don’t like sashimi, and some foreigners do.

That’s true of any kind of food.

Even as Rei said this, Vihera couldn’t help but wear a slightly disappointed expression.

Seeing Vihera like that, Rei spoke up as if a thought had just occurred to him.

“Even if you can’t handle sashimi, maybe you could eat shabu-shabu?”

“Shabu-shabu? Is that strange-sounding name actually a dish?”

“Well, I won’t deny that the name is a bit unusual…”

Compared to dish names like “Roast So-and-so Style,” it’s not surprising that “shabu-shabu” might seem strange.

With that in mind, Rei proceeded to explain what shabu-shabu was.

That said, shabu-shabu isn’t really that difficult to make.

Well, to be precise, if you want to go all out, it probably requires a high level of skill to prepare it properly, but if you think of it as something an amateur can whip up casually, it’s not that difficult—that’s probably the right way to put it.

After all, it’s a dish where you put kelp in a pot to make broth, then briefly dip the sashimi into that broth before dipping it into a sauce to eat.

(The real question is what to use for the dipping sauce, though. Meat tastes great with sesame sauce, but for fish shabu-shabu, ponzu is definitely the best. …And we don’t have soy sauce. Well, worst-case scenario, we could just dip it in the broth from Bistro’s “Decadent Seafood Hot Pot”…)

In fact, if you do shabu-shabu in the seafood hot pot broth, maybe you don’t even need a separate dipping sauce.

Rei wasn’t sure if that would still qualify as proper shabu-shabu, but at the very least, Vihera and the others—who were disappointed they couldn’t eat sashimi—would definitely be able to eat it if it was cooked even a little.

As for Rei, he liked to eat it half-cooked… no, about thirty percent cooked, but with shabu-shabu, he could eat it however he liked.

That way, little by little—really, just a little at a time—they’d get used to the sensation of eating raw fish.

Fortunately—or perhaps I should say—there’s still some of the “Decadent Seafood Hot Pot” soup left over from what Bistol made today.

If that’s the case, it should be fine to use it for shabu-shabu.

“So, we’ll be enjoying seafood shabu-shabu tomorrow night, so don’t worry.”

“Well, ‘rest assured’… I did think it sounded kind of delicious, though.”

After hearing the explanation about shabu-shabu, Vihera understood that if the dish turned out exactly as described, it would undoubtedly be delicious, so she replied as such… and looked delighted that Rei was thinking of them and planning to show off a new dish.
