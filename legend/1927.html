Of the nearly ten adventurers standing in front of the earthen wall, the one who had vented his frustration at Rei ended up unable to say anything more.

Seeing this, Rei decided it would be too much trouble to get involved any further and, without another word, began inspecting the earthen wall alongside Set.

...While the other adventurers present seemed to have plenty they wanted to say to the man and his companion, they likely concluded they weren’t being taken seriously.

Unable to say anything in particular, they fell silent.

In reality, it wasn’t just those who had complaints against Rei; there were actually some who were grateful to him for building the earthen wall. However, after the exchange that had just taken place, they found themselves unable to say anything.

After all, if they were to express their gratitude to Rei right then, there was a chance they’d draw the ire of those who resented him.

“Hmm… From what I can see here, there don’t seem to be any particular problems. Still, the fact that a few Kobolds have managed to get in means they must have found some way to get past this.”

A three-meter-high wall.

Since this isn’t a place an ordinary Kobold could easily scale, it’s possible the ones that got over it weren’t ordinary Kobolds, or perhaps they used their companions as stepping stones by chance…

(Considering the Kobolds the adventurers struggled with yesterday, even if they’re ordinary Kobolds, if they’re veterans… or Kobolds that have lived a long time, it wouldn’t be strange for them to be smart. If only those kinds of Kobolds come over here, that’ll be a hassle in its own way.)

With those thoughts in mind, Rei stood atop the earthen wall and looked across to the other side.

On the other side of the earthen wall, several Kobolds were struggling to climb over it, but they couldn’t manage it.

If it were just an earthen wall, they might have been able to climb over it using their claws.

But this earthen wall had been frozen solid by Meru and Marina’s spirit magic.

So much so that claws as small as a Kobold’s could barely even scratch it.

“Well, even so… it’d be a problem if they managed to get over here. …Flying Slash!”

He pulls the Death Scythe from his Misty Ring and activates the Flying Slash skill.

The flying slash he unleashed cleanly severed the Kobold’s torso. He followed up with several more Flying Slashes, and all the Kobolds standing before the earthen wall were cut in half from top to bottom and killed.

In front of the earthen wall on Gilm’s side, the gathered adventurers could only stare in stunned silence at Rei’s actions.

Judging by Rei’s demeanor, they could probably guess what he had done.

And realizing that they could never do such a thing themselves, they found themselves unable to say a word to Rei.

They had been forced to witness the overwhelming disparity in power.

In particular, the man who had openly voiced his dissatisfaction with Rei regarding the earthen wall—realizing that while he could defeat a Kobold, he could never defeat someone like Rei—stood there with his legs trembling, unable to utter a word.

Rei didn’t seem particularly bothered by the men and left the scene with Set, who had been keeping watch.

“Like I said before, if you want to make money, you should join the Giganto Turtle disassembly. The pay isn’t great, but you’ll get some of the meat, and if you sell that, it’ll fetch a decent price. It’s top-quality meat, so I think you should eat it yourself.”

At those words, a few of the men standing in front of the earthen wall looked up almost reflexively... but Rei paid them no mind and walked away with Set.

“Huh? A wall made of dirt? And that’s why the Kobolds can’t get into Gilm… Is that really true?”

“Yes. That’s correct. Because of that, the Kobolds are heading toward the Giganto Turtle.”

Faced with such an unexpected outcome, the man who heard those words scratched his head in frustration.

Seeing his superior like this, the man who had brought the report made no move, trying as much as possible not to upset him.

He understood that if the man in front of him were so inclined, he could easily kill someone of his own standing.

Scratching his head, in particular, was a clear sign that this man was quite angry.

To avoid incurring his wrath, he had no choice but to remain silent until his boss spoke.

How much time had actually passed in that situation?

In reality, it hadn’t been that long, but to the man in a state of extreme tension, it felt as if an hour had passed.

That’s why, when the boss—who had been scratching his head in irritation—stopped his hand and turned his gaze toward me, I felt a mix of relief and anxiety: “I’m saved,” I thought, “but will I, who brought this report, come out of this unscathed?”

“Oh, the Red Cloth guys—there were still quite a few of them left in Gilm, weren’t there?”

What came out of his boss’s mouth was completely unexpected to the man.

He had been certain his boss was going to say something about the earthen wall.

The remark was so unexpected that the man who had come to report on the earthen walls was at a loss for words...

The next moment, a plate filled with nuts flew through the air, slicing through the atmosphere, and struck the man on the head.

“Gyah!”

“Shut up! I’m asking you a question, so answer me right now!”

“S-sorry. Um…”

Fortunately, since the plate was made of wood, the impact of it hitting his face didn’t cause it to shatter and cut his skin.

Still, the blow caused the man intense pain... Clutching his face, he hurriedly opened his mouth, desperate to avoid further attacks.

“Y-yes. A few have left Gilm, and some have been captured by the guards, but we’ve managed to secure quite a few besides the sacrificial candidates!”

“...Tch, ‘a fair number,’ huh? Did I use up too many for the sacrifices? But since the quality of their souls is lacking, I’ll just have to make up for it with quantity.”

He picks up a nut that had spilled from the plate when he tossed it, pops it into his mouth, and chews it while considering what the best course of action would be.

Since I’m the one in charge of this matter right now, I’d rather not ask the others for help if I can help it.

Well, it would be a different story if I were truly at a loss, but we’re not at that stage yet.

(What the hell is this? My soul’s quality is so low I can only summon Kobolds! And for some reason, there are hardly any that come into town… If only they were higher-rank monsters, I could at least do something about it.)

Just thinking about it makes him furious, but the man must have judged that there’s no point in letting that rage explode right now.

Instead, he decided that dealing with the earthen wall came first, and he should be commended for his foresight in keeping enough of the Red Cloth men alive to use as pawns.

“Tch. I was planning to use those guys as sacrifices when the time came. …Well, whatever. Kobolds aren’t exactly useful for investigating Crimson’s abilities anyway. …Oh well, at least this is some form of harassment. Use the Red Cloth men you can still command to break down that earthen wall.”

"Got it?" Faced with his boss’s intense stare, the man who had come to report could do nothing but nod.

If he tried to defy him here, it wouldn’t be surprising if a dagger—not a plate—came flying at him this time.

“Y-yes, sir! But there aren’t many adventurers at the expansion site right now, so sending too many people might draw attention… How many should I send?”

“Huh? …Well, that depends on how sturdy those earthen walls are… They were built by Crimson, right? If so, sending just one or two people wouldn’t do any good. …Alright, send about twenty.”

“Well, I think it’ll draw attention…”

“Who cares, you idiot. Those guys are nothing but trash who couldn’t even make it as sacrifices. Besides… there’s no evidence linking them to us, right? If they get caught, just think of it as getting rid of some trash.”

Even though they were comrades—no, perhaps he should call them pawns—the man who brought the report couldn’t help but feel something when he heard them so casually dismissed as trash and discarded.

But if asked which was more important—his own life or the Red Cloth men—he would, of course, answer that it was his own.

Given that, he couldn’t very well voice any complaints here.

…After all, if he did, there was a chance he might be told to destroy the earthen wall himself.

“Understood. I’ll do as you say immediately. …Oh, and I’m to just pick out some weapons to give them?”

“That’s right. Without weapons, you wouldn’t be able to break down the earthen wall, after all.”

The two spoke like this, but there was something they didn’t know.

Namely, their belief that the earthen wall was built entirely by Rei.

……Very few people knew that Marina and Meru were using spirit magic to wet the walls with water and freeze it to reinforce them, so that was only natural.

Could the Red Cloth really destroy something that wouldn’t even get a scratch from a Kobold’s claws?

If they had known the details, they probably would have handled the situation a little differently.

But since no one had actually seen them using elemental magic, this difference in perception was unavoidable.

That said, given that Rei created the earthen wall with magic, one could have guessed it wasn’t just an ordinary earthen wall.

“Understood. In that case, I’ll provide plenty of high-damage axes. Also, once the earthen wall is destroyed, there’s a possibility the Kobolds on the other side will attack… but are you okay with the fact that those heading in to destroy it are doing so with the expectation that they might die?”

“Yeah. We keep those good-for-nothings around for times like this. …Seriously, even though it’s a trivial crime, after making them go that far, their souls are still that weak. They really are a bunch of utterly useless scum.”

Seeing his boss growing increasingly displeased with his own words, the man who had come to report hurriedly spoke up.

If he just went off on his own rant, the one who’d suffer the consequences would be him.

Those words were spoken purely to avoid that outcome at all costs.

“If you think of it as the Kobolds taking care of those lot, isn’t that fine? Besides, if the earthen wall gets destroyed and those guys end up getting killed by the Kobolds, it’ll tarnish the reputation of Crimson, who built the wall.”

“Hmph, you’re right.”

The man said only that, then looked toward the door.

With just that, the man who had brought the report understood what his superior meant and bowed his head.

“Then, I’ll have the Red Cloth men destroy the earthen wall immediately. …By the way, what do you plan to do if they survive after the wall is destroyed?”

If they destroyed the earthen wall, they would almost certainly be attacked by a horde of Kobolds.

But the Red Cloth men did, after all, possess the skill to make it to Gilm, however barely.

…In reality, a large number of workers had moved to Gilm for the expansion project, so by blending in with them, most of them had arrived in Gilm safely.

In any case, even among the Red Cloth members—specifically, those who weren’t sacrificed—it wasn’t uncommon to find individuals skilled enough to defeat a Kobold.

That’s precisely why I asked what we should do if we defeated the Kobolds, but all I got from my superior was an irritated glare.

“Those guys are already useless. If I wanted to, I could gather as many of that sort as I please. In other words… you get it, right?”

“You understand, don’t you?” With that question, there was no way I could say I didn’t understand.

Especially since he understood the exact meaning of those words, the man had no choice but to nod.

“Understood. I’ll dispose of them.”

“Do that. Those guys shouldn’t know anything about us, but since we’ve had some dealings with them, we can’t rule out the possibility that they have some information. …Ugh, next time you gather them, make sure they have better souls.”

Even if he was told that, he wouldn’t know what kind of soul that person actually possessed until he actually used them as a sacrifice.

Of course, with those who are clearly unsuitable as sacrifices—like the Red Cloth members currently remaining and those about to be killed—it’s possible to make a reasonable judgment.

“Well, I’ll do my best.”

If I were to answer “I understand” right now, I wouldn’t know what my superior in front of me would do to me if the next people I brought in turned out to be useless.

But if I say I can’t do it, there’s a high chance I’ll be killed right here and now.

In such a situation, the only thing the man could say was, “I’ll do my best…”

...Even within an organization hostile to Gilm, the plight of middle management still exists.
