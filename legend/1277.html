"Pizza, you say? I've never heard of that dish before."

At the word "pizza" coming from Rei's mouth, Rodrigo and Sandrine both tilted their heads in puzzlement.

Though the two didn't look much alike, their mannerisms carried a certain resemblance, befitting parent and child.

"Ah, pizza. ...But this dish isn't rare like meat buns. In terms of cooking, it's just like baking bread."

The idea of steaming bread dough was completely unexpected, even for Rodrigo who ran a cafeteria.

But compared to that, pizza wasn't really that unusual a cooking method.

That said, while the baking method itself wasn't unusual, its appearance was undeniably far more distinctive than your average loaf of bread.

(Plus, it takes less time than baking bread.)

On a TV show Rei saw while in Japan, the dough was placed in the pizza oven and cooked in about a minute.

The oven must be extremely hot to achieve that, but completing cooking in just a minute would definitely benefit a cafeteria by keeping customers from waiting.

...Though, to be clear, Rei was only thinking about the actual baking time. She hadn't given the slightest thought to the prep work involved—rolling out the dough, arranging the various toppings, and so on.

"Will you teach me that too?"

"I plan to. ...But like I said, this isn't some unexpectedly complicated dish like meat buns. It's the kind of thing you could make right away if you just changed your approach a little."

There are probably various formal methods and definitions for making pizza, but Rei naturally knew nothing about that.

That's precisely why what Rei was teaching wasn't formal pizza, but rather a dish that should probably be called pizza-style bread. Still, since pizza doesn't exist as a dish in this world, it would become the formal pizza here.

"Huh. Well, I'd be happy to learn it anyway... But are you sure? I think meat buns are plenty for me."

"Yeah. The pizza thing has to do with my own circumstances."

"Rei's?"

"The method is simple. I figured we could whip it up quickly using the magic item oven while camping. But I don't know how to make the dough or anything like that, so I'd like you to handle that part."

"...I see."

The words coming from Rei's mouth made perfect sense to Rodrigo too.

He knew Rei had ideas about cooking... or knowledge from books, but still, since Rei wasn't a chef, he probably couldn't create new dishes on his own.

Put nicely, it was division of labor; put harshly, it was dumping the task on someone else.

But if that dumping meant he could learn dishes he didn't know, then for Rodrigo, it was actually something he welcomed.

And as a restaurant owner, it was only natural to think, "Maybe we can even expand the menu at the shop?"

"So, what exactly is this dish called pizza?"

Rodrigo asked Rei, his usual quiet demeanor completely gone, his curiosity about this unknown dish clearly visible.

For a moment... just a moment, Rei felt himself being overwhelmed by the sight of Rodrigo now.

"Oh, uh... Well. ...Simply put, it's just thin, round dough topped with various ingredients and baked in an oven. Like a meat bun, the key feature is that you can change the flavor with different fillings. Also, since it's baked directly in the oven, it's ready in about a minute once it goes in."

'One minute!?'

At the words that escaped Rei's mouth, not only Rodrigo but even Sandrine let out a gasp of astonishment in unison.

But that reaction was understandable. From the cafeteria's perspective, a dish that could be served to customers in just one minute was incredibly valuable.

"H-Hey, Rei! Why didn't you tell us about this from the start!? This is way better than meat buns!"

Sandrine, who usually wore a pleasant smile, now glared at Rei with eyes narrowed in disbelief.

...If the customer who had stayed until closing had seen this scene, they would have shot Rei a jealous look.

But luckily for Rei, only Rei, Rodrigo, and Sandrine were present.

"Calm down. I didn't keep the pizza a secret out of spite. Like I said before, making pizza isn't that different from baking regular bread. Compared to steaming dough, it just doesn't surprise customers as much."

"Well... I suppose."

Sandrine was also one of those surprised by the idea of steaming bread.

Rei understood what Rei was trying to say. She understood... but still, she couldn't help wishing Rei would teach her how to make pizza.

As if to soothe Sandrine, Rei continued his persuasion.

"As I explained earlier, making pizza is just placing toppings on thin, round dough and baking it. Honestly, if you're already making regular bread, it wouldn't be strange to make similar bread."

That said, pizza dough and regular bread dough are quite different. So, even if a baker put some thought into it like Rei suggested, what they'd end up with wouldn't be pizza, but more like a pizza imitation... or maybe pizza toast.

Pizza dough, in particular, is broadly categorized into hard-type and soft-type.

Rei liked both, but if they wanted to get it on the menu quickly, the soft type—closer to existing bread dough—was clearly the faster route.

(Well, since we're having Rodrigo research pizza dough, we need to give him some benefit too.)

To be honest, it didn't really matter if it was Rodrigo who researched the pizza for Rei.

He could have approached the Manpukutei restaurant, or perhaps the cafeteria he'd visited several times before, or even gone directly to a bakery due to the oven issue.

Still, the reason I brought it to this shop was probably Rodrigo's personality.

Normally, if someone asked him to research pizza dough just because they wanted to eat it, he'd get angry.

But Rodrigo, while timid... above all, he has a strong curiosity about cooking. I figured he wouldn't get angry even if asked for something like this, like Rei did.

"...I see. So the method itself is simple, huh?"

"Like meat buns, it looks simple, but it's actually surprisingly tricky. First, it's the pizza dough, just like the meat buns. Pizza dough comes in hard and soft varieties. The soft kind is closer to regular bread, so I think we should start with that."

"What kind of dough is the hard type?"

"It's a dough with a crispy, crunchy texture. Because of that texture, I think the soft dough gives you a greater feeling of fullness. Of course, that doesn't mean you won't get full no matter how much of the hard dough you eat."

With Rei's explanation, Rodrigo seemed to grasp the general idea.

Looking satisfied, he moved on to the next topic—the biggest issue with pizza... the toppings.

"As for toppings... just like meat buns, there are all sorts of options."

What flashed through Rei's mind was a flyer for delivery pizza he'd seen in Japan.

They featured photos of countless pizza varieties, each looking incredibly delicious.

...But they were expensive, far beyond what a high school student's allowance could easily afford.

"Isn't there a basic filling, like the meat filling in a meat bun?"

"Hmm... a basic one?"

Rodrigo asked without hiding his interest, and Rei pondered.

(Seafood? Or meat-based ones using sausages? Or maybe just simple ones with herbs and cheese?)

Uncertain how to answer, and eventually unsure what exactly could be called the basic version, Rei explained somewhat tentatively.

"I don't know which one is the basic one, but there are ones that use three ingredients like seafood, meat, and vegetables. Of course, there are all sorts of others besides these three."

"Hmm, since we're making it in Gilm, using seafood might be a bit tricky."

"Yeah, I guess so."

Rodrigo's words sounded genuinely disappointed... truly disappointed from the bottom of his heart. Rei agreed.

In Gilm, seafood meant river fish.

Of course, sea fish also made their way in, but those were mostly dried and preserved for long-term storage.

If someone had a Misty Ring like Rei... or even a degraded item box like the one Elena possessed, that would be a different story. But considering those as magic items, they were extremely rare and expensive.

Indeed, if one were to use such magic items for business, they could be utilized for anything besides fresh fish.

"And vegetables... well, they might be manageable as a side dish, but as a main course, that might be a bit tough."

This was also understandable.

It's winter now, and the vegetables available aren't exactly plentiful.

"True. But some pizzas I know only have herbs as toppings—simple ones."

"Herbs, huh? Well then... Hmm..."

Rodrigo pondered, but since the vegetable issue would resolve itself come spring, it was arguably easier than seafood.

"Ah, and cheese. Basically, pizza needs a lot of cheese. Is that okay?"

"Huh? Oh, yes. Cheese isn't a problem at all. Even in Gilm, while not on a large scale, there are people who raise livestock. And above all, cheese keeps well, so we have plenty of it."

Rodrigo, who had been looking troubled over the seafood and vegetables, exhaled in relief and nodded when he heard "cheese."

"I see. Like I said, there are pizzas made just with herbs and cheese... And then there's tomatoes."

"Tomatoes? That... seems a bit difficult."

Speaking of which, Rodrigo and I were talking, and it occurred to me.

He recalled the vegetables—well, not just vegetables—he'd seen since Rei arrived in Elgin. They were the same things used in Japan, with the same names.

The tomato was one example. Probably, just like Takumu and... the books in the library, it was the legacy left behind by those who came from Japan, like Rei.

Anyway, since saying "tomato" got the point across, it was a huge help for Rei.

If tomatoes hadn't been called tomatoes, he'd have had to explain them as sour red vegetables that ripen in summer.

And then there's the question of whether that explanation would actually get the point across.

Worst of all would have been if Elgin didn't have tomatoes.

Or if they existed but weren't considered edible.

(I remember seeing on TV or something that tomatoes were originally grown as ornamental plants. At least that wasn't the case. ...But still)

Yes, but.

Seeing Rodrigo's troubled expression at Rei's words, and knowing Rei's family background as farmers, Rei realized the biggest problem.

In Japan, greenhouse cultivation means you can eat fresh tomatoes even in winter.

Or, preserved through methods like canning or bottling, so tomatoes are never in short supply year-round.

But this was Elgin.

Preserved foods could be managed to some extent with magic items or spells, but that naturally came at a cost.

At the very least, it wasn't something Rodrigo could afford.

"Ah... The key ingredients for pizza are basically the dough, cheese, and tomatoes..."

If you can't get those crucial tomatoes, you can't make pizza... well, not exactly, but it would definitely get complicated.

(Wait, is that right?)

Thinking it through, Rei suddenly recalled the pizza he'd eaten back in Japan—not from a specialty shop, but something bought at the supermarket.

Did that pizza use fresh tomatoes?

The answer was no.

At least, none of the pizzas Rei had eaten had fresh tomatoes as a topping.

Of course, if it were authentic pizza or from a specialty shop—not the kind that delivers, but the kind baked in a wood-fired oven and served in the restaurant—it wouldn't be strange to use fresh tomatoes even in winter.

But whether it was fortunate or unfortunate, Rei had never eaten that kind of pizza.

"You don't know tomato sauce?"

"Tomato sauce? Of course I know that. We have several bottles at home for cooking too."

Rei had asked, clinging to a faint, desperate hope. But upon hearing that tomato sauce was indeed used in various dishes, she breathed a sigh of relief.

"Good. Then we can make pizza."

"Ah... Rei, wait a moment."

It was Sandrine who called out to stop Rei.

"We do have tomato sauce, but that's meant for other dishes too. That pizza thing, right? We can't use a huge amount of it for that, you know?"

That voice of despair echoed throughout the cafeteria.
